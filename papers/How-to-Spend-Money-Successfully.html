How to Spend Money Successfully

(This paper extends “The Business Model of Life.”)

Life is a business. In this business, your time is the product. Your expenses are your survival costs, and your profit is—ultimately—freedom.

To spend money successfully, you need to optimize for net personal benefit over time. This means seeing every outlay as something that affects your P&L (cash flow) and your balance sheet (assets vs. consumption). Through this lens, the critical question is not, as Suze Orman famously quips, “Can I afford it?” but rather: “Does it strengthen the business, weaken it, or vanish without trace?”

Why? Because this is not about indulging primitive impulses. It’s a simple cost–benefit analysis. Suppress immediacy bias and approach each purchase as a business decision. Ask: Will this purchase make my business (life) measurably stronger or weaker? Does it aid or retard growth? Does it create optionality—or destroy it?

As with most things, emotions cloud our ability to assess value. Emotions ignore depreciation, opportunity cost, and time drag. Financial decisions demand cold, rational assessment. You need to be a mature adult when making a business decision; leave the childish games in the bedroom.

Businesses don’t buy on whim. Neither should you. They buy for purpose. Learn to adapt this model to your daily life.

Map the Terrain

Evaluation begins with classification: Is this consumption or investment?

Investments have persistent value. That value is derived from productivity, increased optionality, and secondary-market liquidity. Consumption consists of things with little or no resale value, low durability, or that are literally consumed as they are used.

Neither consumption nor investment is bad. The problem is conflating the two. Classifying consumption as investment is self-delusion and leads to suboptimal outcomes. Classifying investment as consumption encourages lower quality and less diligence, also producing suboptimal results.

A simple heuristic forces clarity: If it isn’t plausibly resellable at meaningful value, it’s consumption—not investment. This doesn’t ban anything; it forces honesty. It’s also easily testable with a quick search of secondary markets. In most cases, professional-grade durables hold value; consumer-grade items depreciate to near zero almost immediately. That doesn’t mean consumer-grade is always wrong—it’s just more likely to be consumption.

eBay (or equivalent marketplaces) is an excellent resource. Check sold listings before buying anything above a meaningful price threshold. They reveal price floors, liquidity, and demand depth. Estate-sale data confirms the same pattern: tools and commercial items sell; household junk usually ends up as landfill.

The Real Cost of Ownership

Price is an illusion. The true cost is:

discretionary cash outlay
+ time (acquire, maintain, dispose)
+ depreciation
+ opportunity cost
= True cost

Suze Orman’s “Can I afford it?” is the wrong question. The right one is: “How much is this costing me?” This is loaded by design.

Gross income—even take-home pay—is the wrong number. Life-as-a-business reframes this correctly. You sell your time for money. It costs money to live. Your net income is the time and money left over. That’s it.

Calibrate instead to your net discretionary hourly rate:

discretionary income ÷ ~2,000 working hours

Discretionary income is what remains after all living costs and taxes. If you make $50,000 per year and only 10% is discretionary, your true annual profit is $5,000.

That $5 daily designer coffee? It’s not $5 out of $50,000. It’s $5 out of $5,000. Each cup consumes a measurable fraction of your labor. Over a year, $5 × 250 days = $1,250—nearly a quarter of your discretionary capital.

So perhaps the question isn’t “Can I afford it?” but “Is it worth it?”

Luxury and the Need Axis

Luxury isn’t price—it’s deviation from necessity. The less you need something, the more luxurious it is.

Needs fix constraints; preferences boost efficiency; luxuries enhance experience or status. The farther an item is from need, the higher the scrutiny it deserves.

There’s a deep psychology to luxury goods, but the bottom line is this: habitual “treating yourself” creates dependence on treats. Luxury becomes routine. Then, when substitution becomes necessary, it feels demoralizing—beneath you. This is how luxury amplifies regret and psychic drag. Often, luxury serves as emotional justification: a way to rationalize irrational decisions as “therapeutic.”

Durability: Consumer vs. Commercial Grade

There are two broad classes of goods: consumer-grade and commercial-grade.

Consumer-grade products are engineered for intermittent duty. They use smaller components operating under higher stress, resulting in shorter lifespans and minimal resale value. Commercial-grade products are engineered for continuous use. They are overbuilt, operate under lower stress, last longer, and typically have active secondary markets.

Consider stand mixers. Consumer models use smaller motors, higher thermal loads, and short duty cycles. They rely on rest periods for cooling. Professional mixers—like those made by Hobart—use large motors, high mass, conservative ratings, and treat heat as a design constraint. A Hobart can last decades of near-continuous daily use.

Although commercial-grade items often cost more upfront, their consumption rate is far lower. They become capital assets. Ownership cost—not purchase price—matters: longevity, repairability, and liquidity determine the real hit.

Buy a used Hobart mixer for $1–2k, and you can often sell it later for $1–2k. Depreciation effectively disappears; the real cost becomes opportunity cost.

The Obsolescence Exception

Tech-driven categories—electronics and software-tied goods—are exceptions. Even when commercial-grade, they depreciate rapidly and have poor resale value. These should be treated as infrastructure expenses: necessary, predictable depreciation with zero recovery.

Liquidity and Optionality

Resale value isn’t mandatory, but it’s a powerful signal of enduring value. Markets price design intent accurately. Time-to-sell has a cost, but viable exit preserves financial optionality.

Commercial-grade assets also increase optionality by being productive. A blacksmith’s anvil from 1850 is still valuable today because it enables work. The same is true of a commercial stand mixer, even a small one. These tools let you convert time into output.

Putting It Together

Classify correctly. Cost truthfully. Place each purchase on the need–luxury and consumption–investment axes. Most decisions clarify themselves if you’re honest.

Misclassification is an error—and errors compound. Emotional buys become balance-sheet holes. Good purchases compound strength.

Some spending vanishes by design—experiences, food, habits. Price these honestly too.

If you focus on high-quality, resaleable goods, you gradually accumulate a real asset base you can leverage if needed. Most people live in homes filled with things that are functionally worthless.

This framework doesn’t dictate what you should buy. Its purpose is to expose the impact of what you do buy.

Spending 25% of your income on coffee isn’t a poor decision—it’s masochism.
