The Four Decisions That Quietly Decide Your Life

A simple guide to help navigate life’s biggest decisions
(Best read when you’re young: ~13)

If you’re paying attention, you already know that most of life feels noisy and kind of pointless. School, jobs, arguments, money, status, luck — most of the time is routine, boring, and seemingly without meaning. Days pass, patterns repeat, and it’s easy to feel like nothing you do really matters.

Beneath that noise, however, lurk a small number of very big decisions that quietly dominate all others. You may not realize it yet, but the constant noise of daily life biases you against your own long-term self-interest. If you want to live a long, happy, and successful life, you need to get a small number of things right — not perfectly, just structurally right.

Not because they happen first, and not because they feel dramatic, but because they lock in risk.

For most people, the four decisions that matter most are simple to name:

  1. Who you marry

  2. Whether you have children

  3. What home you live in

  4. What car you drive

These decisions are not equal. They do not carry equal risk, and they do not forgive mistakes at the same rate. That’s the bad news. The good news is that they can be ranked.

The Ranking (by Risk, Not Timing)

When ranked from highest risk to lowest risk, the list looks like this: spouse, children, home, then car.

This ranking is not a moral judgment; it is a statement of structural fact. Who you choose to marry is a foundational choice. Where you choose to live is also a foundational choice. The life you build — your future — is quite literally set upon these decisions.

Failures made early in life are typically easier to recover from. Failures made later, especially in foundational areas, compound much faster. What matters most is not when a decision is made, but how large and irreversible it is.

A bad car choice is painful — it’s expensive and annoying. A bad house choice is worse, because it becomes a geographic and financial constraint that limits opportunity. But a bad husband or wife? The wrong life partner redefines the arc of your future. It removes agency and destroys optionality by taking away control. The right choices do the opposite.

A Unifying Principle

All four decisions share the same underlying structure. They involve asymmetric downside, limited upside, false narratives that obscure risk, and overconfidence driven by novelty — whether that novelty is lust, envy, or status.

Structurally, they are all the same decision tree. What differs is how permanent the damage is when things go wrong.

Cars: The Simplest Case

When you strip away the stories people tell themselves, a car is a lifestyle expense. It is not an investment.

The moment you take possession, it legally becomes a used car. It begins to depreciate immediately. It starts costing money to maintain, store, insure, and operate. Your outcome is dominated by two variables: the price you paid and the steepness of the depreciation curve.

The correct question is not “What do I want?” or “How cool will this make me look?” The correct question is: How easily can I walk away from this liability?

A useful guideline is that the purchase price of your car should not exceed 3–5% of your net worth. The reason is simple: your car should be something you can abandon without damaging your life trajectory.

If that number sounds impossible, it’s because your perspective is misaligned. Used cars can be acquired for a few hundred dollars. They are not pretty or impressive, but they work. Focus on utility. As your wealth rises, so too will your budget and the quality of the car you can afford. A car is not an extension of yourself unless you allow it to be. If you feel social pressure, the correct response is simply, “It does what I need it to do.”

When buying a car, optimize for the depreciation curve.

Homes: A Lifestyle Expense, Not an Investment

Like a car, a home is also a lifestyle expense. Once built or purchased, it immediately begins to decay and depreciate. This does not mean you cannot make money on a home — markets fluctuate and leverage exists — but for most people, once you factor in all costs, especially opportunity cost, a home is one of the most expensive decisions they will ever make.

The best advice here is to ignore what other people do and tell you.

A home is a concentration of capital. It reduces flexibility, increases fixed costs, anchors you to a location, and requires income stability. These are not moral flaws, but they are real constraints.

A prudent guideline is to limit the value of your home to roughly 10–20% of your net worth. For most people, that sounds unrealistic — and that’s the point. With time, saving, and discipline, what feels impossible at 25 often becomes reasonable at 45. Until then, renting preserves optionality. You can walk away from an apartment with little penalty. Walking away from a house is slow, expensive, and damaging.

Life-changing opportunities — meeting the right partner, landing the right job — are rare and highly sensitive to time and place. Optimize for flexibility. Treat appreciation as a bonus, never a justification. If you need your home to “work out” financially, you have already lost the argument.

Children: An Aspirational Luxury

Biologically, reproduction is a core human function, but the incentives surrounding children have changed. In earlier eras, children were an investment: they provided labor, continuity, and social insurance. That world no longer exists.

In the modern age, uncomfortable as it sounds, children are best understood as a luxury expense. This is not a judgment; it is a classification. Luxury does not mean bad. Luxury means optional, expensive, largely intangible in value, and carrying permanent downside risk. Financially, the return on investment is negative.

When framed correctly, children become aspirational rather than obligatory. Luxury chosen consciously is empowering and deeply appreciated. Luxury assumed without capacity is crushing.

Spouse: The Most Dangerous Decision You Will Ever Make

Marriage is not primarily about romance, emotion, or hope. It is a legal contract between you, your spouse, and the government you live under. In practical terms, it is a business partnership in the enterprise called life.

Your spouse determines risk tolerance, conflict resolution, financial posture, stress behavior, and how failure is handled. Physical attraction is biological and front-loaded; it will decay with time. People age — that is constant. How they age is the variable.

Beauty today can become ugly tomorrow. Ugly today may become beauty tomorrow.

What matters most is functional compatibility, and that value is almost always back-loaded. It compounds over time. If aesthetic enjoyment is your primary selection criterion, you are optimizing for early pleasure while locking in later regret.

Approach partner selection the way you would any other high-stakes contract. Inspect the foundation. Read the terms. Stress testing should include brutal financial honesty, direct engagement with value conflicts, explicit discussion of power dynamics, and realistic failure scenarios. This must happen before legal commitment. If both partners are rigid in opposite directions, the shared future is empty.

This is not pessimism. It is math.

Honesty does not negate love; it strengthens it. Projecting a false persona to secure a deal almost guarantees failure.

Degradation and Growth

Some values decay with time, while others grow. Aesthetics fade. Familiarity can increase utility. Stability compounds. Misalignment accelerates failure. Wise decisions favor back-loaded value over front-loaded pleasure. This is why cars, homes, children, and partners are not interchangeable.

Final Rule

You cannot eliminate risk. You can only decide where it lives. Put reversible risk early. Put irreversible risk last.

Never confuse lifestyle with destiny.

Everything else is commentary.
