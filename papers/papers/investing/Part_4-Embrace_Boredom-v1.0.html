Embrace Boredom

We all want the chemical highs of winning. We all hate the lows from losing. But here’s the thing: we are hard-wired to viscerally hate boredom. Boredom is the worst.

How bad is boredom? Clinical studies have actually been conducted where they lock people in a room with a button. Pushing the button causes pain—mild electric shocks to the ankle or wrist. Given enough time, people will engage in purposeful self-harm just to cure the agony of boredom. In one famous experiment from the University of Virginia (published in Science in 2014), nearly half the participants shocked themselves at least once during 15 minutes alone, with some men doing it dozens or even hundreds of times. They’d rather feel pain than sit with their own thoughts and nothing to do. Follow-up studies (Maastricht University, 2016) showed boredom specifically drives this behavior more than sadness or neutral states, and people with a history of non-suicidal self-injury were even quicker and more intense about it. The conclusion is clear: boredom is so aversive that many prefer physical discomfort to the emptiness.

Now… how the hell does this apply to investing? Well, I got bad news for you: this is a seriously boring job.

Why? Because real opportunity is rare.

Your job—if you want to do this seriously—is to constantly, patiently, methodically search for those rare moments and act only on those rare moments. That’s it. The rest is waiting.

Being fully invested all the time keeps the sell-side fed (commissions, spreads, ideas, narratives) and makes your life much harder. You only need a few good trades in your life to get super rich. The key is to embrace boredom. Learn to love it. Find hobbies, learn, read, expand your mind while you patiently wait for something to do.

Bought a stock? Great. Enjoy being bored owning it.

Richard Russell of Dow Theory Letters once brilliantly noted that the difference between a rich person and a poor person is really quite simple: the rich person can afford to wait.

What does this mean? The rich person doesn’t need to be invested to get rich. He or she can sit on his or her hands and do nothing for a very long time, waiting for opportunity to come to them. Then they act.

A poor person? Well, they need money to keep the lights on and the kids fed, so they don’t wait. They try to make opportunity. The problem is, no matter how hard you try, you can’t go to the fish—you must let the fish come to you.

Trying to force trades because you’re bored is like going fishing with dynamite. You might catch something, but you’re more likely to blow up your pond, scare everything away, and end up with nothing but regret.

The emotional cup ties in here directly. Boredom empties the cup slowly—chronic low-level drain. You feel restless, antsy, like you’re “missing out.” The cup drops below 4, and suddenly you’re rationalizing marginal ideas, increasing size on mediocre setups, or revenge-trading after a small loss just to feel something. The boredom becomes its own emotional trigger, pushing you into stupid action.

The antidote isn’t forcing excitement. It’s learning to tolerate—and eventually enjoy—the emptiness.

If you’re wired as a spender/trader type (from the last paper), boredom is torture. Your brain craves stimulation, action, dopamine. Sitting still feels like death. You’ll invent reasons to trade because waiting hurts more than losing sometimes.

If you’re wired as a saver/value type, boredom might feel more natural—but prolonged cash drag or “nothing happening” can still erode you into apathy, second-guessing, or premature selling just to feel productive.

Either way, the market doesn’t reward hyperactivity. It rewards the person who can sit through years of nothing and still be ready when the dislocation arrives.

Embrace boredom means:

  • Stop measuring your worth by how many trades you make or how “active” you look.

  • Build a life outside markets so waiting doesn’t feel like punishment.

  • Treat cash as a position—not dead money, but ammunition.

  • When the cup starts emptying from inactivity, recognize it as a signal to reset, not to act.

  • Remember: the rich person waits. The poor person chases.

You don’t get rich by being busy. You get rich by being right a few times, at scale, and having the discipline to wait between those times.

Boredom isn’t the enemy. It’s the price of admission for the rare moments that actually matter. Learn to love the wait, or the market will teach you the hard way.
