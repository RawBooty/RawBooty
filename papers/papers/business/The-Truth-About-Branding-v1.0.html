Branding: Learn to Make Friends, Not Pictures

(Understanding Identity Through Evolutionary Psychology)

Fun Fact: Company brands didn’t exist until the 19th century. Ivory Soap (1879) was among the first true modern brands—named, nationally advertised, with claims of purity and the famous “It Floats.” Before that, people bought soap from the guy who makes soap.

Ever wonder why we have last names like Shoemaker, Smith, Tailor, Baker? In small towns, your name plus your job was your identity. That was your brand.

Incidentally, the word brand originally meant a mark burned into cattle to designate ownership. Early branding was simple. The message: “this cow is mine.”

Modern branding is the same thing—except now it’s a claim on your mind.

For over 300,000 years of human history, there were no logos. No taglines. No brand guidelines. Humans lived in small groups and knew each other directly. We did not evolve with abstract corporate entities, and our brains still have no native slot for them.

What we do have is wiring for people: names, faces, personalities, relationships. That wiring explains why people form deep emotional bonds with brands like Disney or Harley-Davidson.

To build a brand that actually works, you need to understand one thing:

Customers don’t experience your brand as a thing. They experience it as a meta-person. A pseudo-human. This isn’t metaphor. It’s developmental and evolutionary psychology. Identity isn’t rational—it’s tribal. Your brain treats brands like people because that’s the only system it has.

The core realization: there is only one way to build a lasting brand—make it a person.
Give it a name. A personality. Quirks. Utility. Emotional hooks that resemble human relationships.

Everything else is forgettable noise.

Definitions

The Brand as Person

Your brand occupies a slot in the customer’s mental social network. Research suggests humans can recognize and remember roughly 5,000 faces and names (estimates range from 1,000 to 10,000). This is not infinite—it’s a hard cognitive limit inherited from our hunter-gatherer past. Training can expand it slightly, but most people cap out.

That’s 5,000 total.
Not 5,000 people and 5,000 brands.

Think about it this way: Do you allocate a slot to remember your best friend’s kid’s name… or someone else’s company? That’s the friction you’re fighting.

Your brand isn’t competing for “mindshare.” It’s competing for one of those finite slots, the same way a friend, rival, or enemy would. If your brand doesn’t feel like a person, it gets dropped. Personality trumps aesthetics. And yes—the word “Trump” does a lot of work.

A brand’s personality isn’t fluff. It’s the traits that form bonds: reliable (a dependable friend),
bold (a charismatic leader), shocking (that unforgettable jerk you met in college).

Logos and colors are just the face. The real work is identity. What does this “person” stand for?
How does it make people feel? Utility and emotion seal the deal.

Apple isn’t a computer company. Steve Jobs built a friendly, sexy, rebellious innovator who empowers creators. Nike isn’t footwear—it’s a relentless motivator whispering “just do it.”

These are the virtual people you “know” and trust.

The Scoring System

(This Is Where People Get Confused)

  • Weak person → your brand fades (forgettable, like naming your kid John)

  • Solid person → loyalty (customers “know” you; repeat business)

  • Iconic person → dominance (one of those 5,000 slots locked forever)

Most brands fail because they think a clever name or pretty design is enough. It isn’t. Without personality, you’re not a person—you’re wallpaper. And wallpaper isn’t remembered.

Think about it: how many ads can you recall where you remember the product or the joke, but not the brand? That’s why.

Brands aren’t inventions. They’re evolutionary hacks. Ignore the marketing gurus selling “brand strategy” as modern art. This is Psychology 101.

Humans evolved to track alliances: who’s useful, who’s dangerous, who’s trustworthy. Brands hijack that circuitry. A successful brand does what a human name does—instant recognition, emotional association, memorability.

Common names blend in. Unique ones stick—even when controversial.

George Foreman named all his kids George. He called it a gift: instant memorability in a sea of forgettables.

Names come with embedded context you can hijack.

About “rawbooty”

Shocking? Offensive? Absolutely.

Memorable? Unavoidably.

“rawbooty” checks the boxes and captures a slot and squats there—especially once you understand the definition and the purpose.

Was it comfortable to adopt? No. But we’re in good company. Consider Kum & Go, Hooker Furniture, Big Ass Fans. The world is full of cheeky, abrasive brands that stick because neutrality doesn’t.

People don’t remember “rawbooty” because it’s polite. They remember it because it has a personality: raw, unfiltered, provocative. That’s the edge.

The Real Mistake

Treating branding as visuals or slogans is a mistake. “Let’s get a cool logo” isn’t the answer. That’s like dressing a corpse—it looks nice, but it doesn’t breathe. Worse is bland neutrality: medium personality, average utility, no emotional pull. You end up as that acquaintance people vaguely recognize but never call. In saturated markets, this kills you faster than a bad product. Competitors with stronger personalities steal your slots.

Constraints matter more than creativity. You don’t freely choose your brand’s personality—your business forces it.

  • Solo operator? Your brand is you: gritty, human, authentic.

  • Scaling fast? Your brand must become a consistent person systems can maintain.

  • Niche market? Be hyper-specific—the expert friend for that tribe.

Fake it, and it collapses.

A “fun” brand run by a joyless founder rings hollow. Customers feel the mismatch immediately. Trust dies fast and resurrects slowly.

Execution Beats Inspiration

Execution means living the personality. Every tweet, email, product decision, and interaction reinforces it. Building a virtual person is hard. Customers test brands the way they test friends—through screwups, delays, stress, and success. Pass the tests and earn loyalty. Fail, and you’re ghosted.

Memorability has costs. Shock risks backlash, filters, shadow bans. Utility builds slow trust. Emotional traits demand vulnerability—brands that claim to care must actually deliver.

None of this is easy. Choose what fits, choose what you think you can execute. Just don’t choose rawbooty – it’s already taken.

It’s a game of psychological warfare for those 5,000 slots. In the trade this is called “mindshare”.

Bottom Line

There’s really only one way to reliably build a successful brand: make it a person.

Humans see meta-people, not abstractions. Give your brand a memorable name, a distinct personality, real utility, and emotional gravity.

And don’t kid yourself: if you can’t compete on utility, price, or service, branding won’t save you. Acme Flypaper still wins if it’s a better quality product, that costs less, and is back by excellent customer service.

No brand ever overcomes that. Respect your limits.
