Marketing: Don’t Burn Your Friends

(What Not to Do When You Finally Have Something Worth Selling)

You’ve nailed market fit.
You’re winning (or damn close) on one or two of the three axes.
Your brand feels like a person.
There’s cash in the tank.

Time to market.

Bad news: 99% of what passes for “marketing” is wasted money.

TV. Radio. Paid social. Agencies. You’d get better ROI wiring the cash to a stranger and asking them to kick you in the ass personally.

Why does McDonald’s advertise nonstop? Not to build brand—they already own the mindshare slot for fast burgers. Their ads defend frequency, not awareness: think burger → feel hungry → buy. Coca-Cola does the same thing. Omnipresent reminders timed to thirst.

Incumbents defend frequency because awareness is already locked.

Your problem is different—and harder.

As a new or small player, you’re fighting uphill on two fronts:

  • Awareness: no one knows you exist

  • Education: if your offer solves something complex, people don’t even know they need it

This paper isn’t a how-to playbook. That’s a library, not a lesson.

This is the first and most important rule: What not to do.

Because mindshare is brutally hard to earn—and ridiculously easy to lose.

Marketing Is Relationship Management

Warren Buffett put it plainly during the Salomon Brothers crisis:

“Lose money for the firm, and I will be understanding. Lose a shred of reputation for the firm, and I will be ruthless.”

The subtlety in this quote is where the value lives. Your job as a marketer isn’t to sell. It’s to earn—or keep—a friend. This follows directly from our branding paper: your brand is a pseudo-person. Marketing is how that person behaves toward real people.

Pester a friend constantly? They block you.

Act desperate? They ghost—or worse, exploit you.

(Sad, but human.)

Practically speaking: when someone gives you their email, phone number, or attention, that’s trust. Treat it like it’s scarce and precious- because it is.

Most companies grind for years, spending a fortune, building distribution lists, then light them on fire with the logic: “1M emails = $1M sales. Low cost, high ROI.”

Short-term bump. Long-term incineration of precious goodwill. Spam filters tighten. Engagement collapses. Trust evaporates. What took 10–20 years to earn dies in weeks for incremental cash.

A real friend only reaches out with value—relevant, timely, useful.

Scale that:

  • Infrequent contact

  • Small, targeted segments

  • Personalize where possible

  • Always lead with utility

Not “BUY NOW” desperation.

Nike says Just Do It.
We say Don’t do it.

Another Deadly Trap: Discounts and Sales

Discounts don’t earn customers. They attract opportunists and parasites. They weaken your brand and punish the loyal customers who paid full price.

Price-chasers buy only on deal. They churn fast and leave zero loyalty. The 80:20 rule applies. 80% of your profit usually comes from 20% of your customers. Don’t burn the 20 to grow the 80. This is bad business.

Frequent cuts broadcast one message: “We can’t sell at value.”

Frequent sales turn your pseudo-person (your brand) into the desperate acquaintance begging for money.

Your real customers notice this behavior and feel taken advantage of: “I paid full price last month. Now it’s 40% off for strangers?” this erodes trust. Referrals dry up. Defection follows.

If you must discount—clear inventory, seasonal pressure, pricing tests—do it carefully:

  • Reward your best customers privately (VIP perks, early access, gratitude)

  • Dump excess through hidden channels (outlets, wholesalers, flash sites your core audience doesn’t track)

  • Keep it rare and unpredictable—never habitual

Better alternatives:

  • Bundles

  • Free add-ons

  • Loyalty rewards

  • Referrals

Add value without slashing price.

Discounts train customers to wait for more discounts. Is this your goal?
Discounts also devalue perceived quality. It cheapens your value proposition.

Discounts shift customer loyalty from brand personality and utility to price alone.

This is fragile and self-destructive.

The Scoring System

(Where People Screw Up)

  • Burn friends fast (blasts + broad discounts) → short-term cash, long-term death

  • Respect boundaries → steady, compounding loyalty

  • Become indispensable → defensible moat (rare, enduring)

The best marketing advice is brutally simple:

Be a better friend.

This is really all you have to do to win business.

Offer value first.
Sell second—or never overtly.

Constraints Matter

Low budget? Prioritize earned and organic: referrals, useful content, word of mouth.

High budget? Same rule applies. Ads, when done right, amplify real friendships; they almost never create them from strangers.

Execution Beats Tactics

Funnels, retargeting, A/B tests—these are tools. The problem remains that desperate behavior kills faster than bad tactics. Over-blasting, public fire sales, constant promos accelerate failure.

Live the friend personality in every touchpoint.

Bottom Line

Marketing preserves and deepens the relationship your brand (pseudo-person) has already earned.

One bad blast, one public discount, one desperate grab for cash.

That’s all it takes to torch years of goodwill and hard work.

Respect your customers time, protect their privacy, and reward them with the attention a real friend deserves. Always lead with utility to your customer; do not look to find utility in them.

And remember: if you can’t win on price, quality, or service, no marketing will save you.

But if you can—don’t sabotage the mindshare you fought so hard to earn.

It’s a game of psychological warfare for those 5,000 slots.
In the trade, this is called mindshare.
