What Is Your Purpose?
Your very existence hinges on this question. Can you answer it?

Calibration
The average, healthy human walks at a pace of roughly three to four miles per hour; perhaps 20–30 miles on a good day. At this pace, crossing the United States would take ~3 months, the universe ~3.6 trillion times longer than the universe is thought to have existed. Zoom out even just a little in cosmic scale and our entire solar system vanishes from perspective, lost to the vastness of the universe.

Cosmically, no one can ever matter. But this is the wrong perspective by which to measure yourself. The only perspective that exists for you is your own lived experience. Significance does not exist universally; it exists locally, within you. Why then does universal scale matter? Because, when judged from any perspective other than your own, nothing you do really matters very much at all. Judged from your perspective, everything, no matter how small, takes on a special—to you and only you—significance.

This is not a contradiction. It is an important boundary condition.

Simply stated: if meaning does not exist externally, it must be generated by you, internally. If you seek it externally, you surrender control to the randomness of life. If, on the other hand, you accept that it must be generated by you, then it must obey some biological and structural rules. This gives you agency: control, power over your future. You control your perception of perspective, if you so choose.

Purpose is not a gift. It is not a discovery. It is not a calling. It is a mechanism—and like any mechanism, it has non-negotiable operating requirements, requirements rooted in a billion years of evolutionary pressure. In the very real sense, you are the product of your collective past.

This knowledge is empowering.

“Find Your Passion”
One of the single most corrosive pieces of advice ever told to any young person is “find your passion.” It is lazy, dismissive, and deeply harmful. Why? Because it teaches the idea that purpose is a pre-existing object, a destiny hidden, waiting to be uncovered or revealed. It negates the need for self-motivation; meaning arrives if you search or wait long enough. It strips you of your dignity, robs you of your potential, it denies your will to take control.

You do not “find” passion. You do not “find” purpose. Purpose must be actively constructed through sustained engagement with difficulty. Passion is not the cause of meaningful work; it is a byproduct of self-validation. This validation only comes from consistently doing work competently over a period of time.

Telling people to wait for passion? This all but guarantees personal stagnation. Worse, for most who follow this advice it produces boredom, anxiety, despair, and depression. Purpose does not appear first. Effort does. Struggle does. Without action, there is no feedback. No awareness of success or failure. Without action you exist in a state of nothingness.

Purpose Is a Biological Necessity
Dopamine is a chemical produced by the primitive brain. It exists in most animals; its purpose is counterintuitive as it does a few things through a simple mechanism. The simplest way to think about it is as a kind of emotional, or mental, fuel. A bit like food. And like all food, it has a limited shelf life. Dopamine collected today will slowly rot and decay over time. Buy too much food, stuff it in the fridge, and what you wind up with is a disgusting, depressing mess you eventually need to clean up.

Serotonin on the other hand is a reward; like a candy bar, or an orgasm. It’s intense, sudden, but brief.

The critical point here is that the human brain, your brain, has no stable idle state. It exists in one of only two states: progresses or decay.

This is not a statement of philosophy or willpower. It is involuntary biology, over which you have no control. No matter how hard you try you will always age. You must always sleep and inactivity will always make you depressed.

Dopamine is not happiness. It is not pleasure. Dopamine is brain food—fuel for motivation, anticipation, persistence. It functions as time decay between task completion. Dopamine decays continuously without reinforcement from progress, challenge overcome, or visible improvement.

Serotonin is the reward—the “happy spike” after completion, the fleeting satisfaction. But serotonin cannot bridge time gaps or sustain effort. It is transient, builds tolerance, provides no direction.

Finding bliss? This is another way of saying you should “chase serotonin,” which is fleeting.
Purpose on the other hand is chasing dopamine—persistent, process-based, renewable only through progress. Purpose is hunting or growing your own food, not sitting around consuming someone else’s.

No purpose? No dopamine? The brain starves to death.

From a mental health perspective, dopamine renewal is quite literally existential. Without it, motivation collapses, time feels heavy, agency erodes, sanity degrades. Yes, drugs can repress this—mask the decay—but they cannot change the fact that dopamine is deficient. The biological mechanism persists. This causes premature aging and death.

Why Are We Like This?
Evolution explains this mechanism quite cleanly. Laziness was a death sentence for most of the 300,000 years humans are thought to exist. If you didn’t plan for winter, gather enough wood, food, build a warm shelter… you froze to death, starved to death, or were abandoned by your peers. There was no passive survival. Inaction was lethal. Survival required a mechanism that motivated action.

The solution to this problem was the brain evolving a punishment for stagnation, one with escalating discomfort. In short, happiness has a very real biological time decay. Depression sucks, for a reason: it’s functional—like pain. Depression is your body getting sad because it thinks the mind is going to cause it to die for lack of effort.

Wish you didn’t feel pain? This actually exists; it’s a rare condition called “Congenital Insensitivity to Pain (CIP).” It’s a rare genetic disorder in which pain-sensing nerve cells (nociceptors) fail to develop or function properly. Children born with this condition do things normal kids do like chew their fingernails… only they don’t know where to stop. They keep going, eventually eating their own fingers, bone and all. Children born without pain perception self-mutilate catastrophically, and typically die young.

You may not like pain, you may not like depression; but you owe your very existence to both.

Humans were built to survive nature's challenges—scarcity, resistance, uncertainty—not luxury's void. Remove survival pressures, and the machinery misfires. Luxury does not liberate; it exposes the brain's inability to idle without decay.

The miracle of our biology is that pain avoidance drives everything. We are taught pleasure motivates us, but that's a lie. The truth is darker: no one wants pain—physical, emotional, existential—so we fight to avoid it. Pleasure is optional and weak; it’s never enough to move the truly lazy. Nature had to break a fundamental law of physics: energy takes the path of least resistance. Pain forces us to take the harder path. Passion is what develops when we master that resistance—not before.

Pain avoidance is what motivates. It is, in a very real sense, what drives us toward purpose. Not the pursuit of pleasure, but the stable condition of avoiding pain.

Pleasure is episodic, habituates quickly, provides no sustained direction. Pain is structural: it persists until the condition is resolved. Humans optimize for survivability under pain avoidance, not happiness.

A viable life is one that learns to tolerate calibrated pain through forward motion, visible progress, earned competence. To maximize the satisfaction you get from life, you just seek out the optimal level of resistance / pain that’s right for you. This doesn’t mean you seek misery; it means you seek difficult, but not existential challenges. Do something that’s not easy, accomplish your goal and happiness will be an incidental byproduct. Always choose to do that which is easy, and happiness will be absent entirely.

The goal is not bliss. The goal is motion that keeps pain productive, motivating, not destructive.

Struggle Is the Only Substrate for Purpose
What few realize is that struggle is the only substrate for purpose. Purpose cannot exist in an idle state. It only exists where there is progress or decay. Risk is what motivates; it’s what keeps life interesting.

Now to be clear, struggle is not some romanticized version of suffering or chaos. It is voluntary, purposeful engagement with resistance. Struggle is measured in difficulty and it scales for capacity. Think of it like a video game: at level 1 killing a mouse is an accomplishment, at level 50 killing a mouse is pointless. Why? It accomplishes nothing in terms of meaningful progress. Risk of failure is what makes the activity fun. Uncertainty of outcome is what births engagement.

Purpose requires three co-occurring elements:

  1. Resistance—something pushes back; effort demanded.

  2. Agency—you can act and influence outcomes.

  3. Progress—measurable improvement over time.

On the surface, it's a simple heuristic. But look a little deeper and it’s pure biological necessity. If there’s no resistance then effort is pointless. If effort is pointless dopamine goes unreplenished. Without agency, helplessness loops. No progress results in emotional starvation, even if competent and engaged.

Of the three conditions, Condition 3 dominates because dopamine is tuned to delta (change), not absolute state. Change is what the mind monitors and measures. If there’s no delta, then there’s no refill of dopamine and decay progresses. You must always seek a condition which enables self-improvement. This is existential.

The strongest predictor of sustainability are mastery gradients. These are things that offer long-term opportunities for improvement.

Too little struggle → decay (boredom, apathy). Too much → trauma (breakdown). The viable band is narrow but identifiable. Passion develops as you discover the boundary of your limits, and learn to push the boundary further than you once believed possible.

If you do not choose a struggle, your mind chooses: anxiety, rumination, addiction, self-sabotage. The mechanism is involuntary.

The Operational Heuristic: Testable Conditions for Purpose Generation
Purpose generates when work satisfies:

  1. You are competent enough—too hard → demoralization.

  2. It engages intellectually/emotionally—boredom decays faster than pain.

  3. You improve over time—no progression → system failure.

This heuristic is fractal and testable:
• Surface—pick work meeting criteria.
• Deeper—monitor dopamine signals (motivation sustained? Time bearable?).
• Biological tie-in: Progression refills fuel; without, starvation inevitable.

Apply iteratively: Start small, measure delta, adjust. Nuance: Early aesthetic enjoyment front-loaded, decays fast. Functional value compounds with familiarity, syncing.

Wealth, Tools, and the Retirement Delusion: Never Quit, Always Advance
Money is a tool.
Wealth is a surplus of tools.
Tools enable change; but they do not create meaning.

The goal of retirement—especially early retirement—is delusional.
To retire means to quit from productive life. You never want to quit being productive. You must learn to remove "retirement" from your mind; train your mind to see this word as derogatory. Quitting removes resistance without replacement, guaranteeing decay. Having retirement as a goal is like wishing to die young—psychologically; it often results in premature death. (This is documented fact, not opinion.)

Early retirement inverts natural incentives: it removes necessity, replaces it with an empty void.

You may not like change, but your mind requires it. It’s what keeps you young and healthy. Change must be earned. Change is only rewarding if it is advancing to higher purpose: more rewarding, interesting, leveraged. Never optimize for stopping. Optimize for progression into self-directed challenge.

Closing: Choose Struggle or It Chooses You; Purpose Is Manufactured
You cannot escape struggle. What you must do is choose the form of struggle you engage with. You can struggle with purpose; or you can struggle with depression. Both scenarios are painful; only one is rewarding.

Purpose is the mechanism that enables you to convert pain to productive motion—not destructive despair.

Final point:
Humans were built to survive nature's challenges, not luxury's emptiness. Meaning is manufactured through resistance, agency, and progress.

Biologically, dopamine production is existential. This is why people climb mountains, jump from airplanes, and build companies. Embrace your humanity.

Your goal should not be happiness. The goal must be sustained forward motion while avoiding existential pain.

This is purpose.
