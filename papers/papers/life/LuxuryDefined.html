How to Think About Luxury

A guide to understanding surplus, choice, and the subtle rules that govern value

Let’s begin with a seemingly simple question: what is “luxury”? If you’re like most people, you might think it’s driving a Mercedes, owning a Birkin bag, traveling to exotic locations, or having a big house. While those examples are true, they are not correct in principle, nor are they the optimal way to rationalize this important concept.

The pursuit of luxury can be constructive, destructive, or, oddly enough, constructively destructive. Outcome and value in this instance are deeply personal and highly dynamic as you move through life. It is for this specific reason that the way you handle luxury matters more than you probably realize. For starters, luxury is not wealth. Luxury is not access. Luxury is not even a reward. Luxury is a personal optimization of your time and resources. Your goal should be to seek a balance between what you have, what you need, and what you can comfortably afford to spend. “Comfort” here means that the purchase causes no stress—neither at the moment of purchase nor for the remainder of your life afterward.

The Nature of Luxury

At its most basic, luxury exists when you have either: a) more than you need, or b) better quality than conditions require.

Think of it this way: if you use resources beyond the base requirements of survival or minimal function, then you are experiencing luxury. A drill is a luxury compared to a screwdriver. A screwdriver is a luxury compared to a nail. A nail is a luxury compared to a wooden dowel, and so on. Luxury is always relative. In medieval times, growing ornamental grass was serious luxury. Only people who could afford to dedicate land and labor to unproductive grass—rather than crops—did so. This was a functional, purposeful demonstration of surplus resources and power. It signaled that if you could afford to waste capacity like this, you could also afford to fight. This signal commanded respect.

Today, the principle is the same; only the medium has changed. Cars, homes, clothes, leisure, and even ideas serve as modern expressions of luxury.

Critical Truths About Luxury

  • Luxury is highly context-dependent. Time, energy, and resources exist on gradients. More possessions often mean less freedom. Fewer possessions often mean more control over your time and more freedom to move about.
  • Luxury is dual in nature. It can be consumptive, bringing personal satisfaction, or expressive, sending signals to others about your capacity to waste or consume. The optimal form is usually private, non-public luxury, where optionality is maximized and risk of external pressure is minimized.
  • Luxury is temporal. Its value may grow or decay over time, depending on use, context, and personal skill in managing it.
  • Luxury is personal. Beyond a certain point, it becomes a subjective state, and for some, a failed state. Owning a gigantic house or an endless collection of items can consume your time and attention, turning freedom into burden.

Illustrative Examples

Imagine a house. A small house is easy to fix, clean, and manage—you can control it without help. As the house grows, your ability to maintain it rapidly diminishes. Beyond a certain point, say a few thousand square feet, the house requires so much care and attention that hired help becomes a necessity. At this point, the house begins to dominate your life rather than serve it.

The same holds true for clothes, cars, or even schedules filled with choices. Eventually, the relationship inverts, and luxury turns into burden or a time sink. Where this point occurs is highly individual. As a result, luxury is a personal price curve. Optimization requires a dual state of optionality: you must have both the resources and the freedom to deploy them. Without one or the other, what looks like luxury can quickly become misery.

Life’s a Stage

Luxury also has a theatrical value. Displaying it can signal competence, taste, or power. But the signal is subtle: too public, and it invites challenge or provocation; too hidden, and it fails to leverage social benefit. Too ostentatious, and it can attract resentment or contempt.

Successful management of luxury requires balancing visibility with discretion, maintaining control while sending signals only when useful. Not all luxury is public, and not all signals are meant to impress. The most powerful form of luxury is non-public, optional, and temporally flexible. It is the ability to live freely, act without coercion, and allocate your attention to what matters to you—not others. Few people ever attain this.

Consumptive vs. Expressive Luxury

  • Consumptive luxury is for your own satisfaction: a meal, a book, a private retreat.
  • Expressive luxury is for others: an expensive car, a visible home, a fashionable outfit.

Both have value and purpose, but conflating the two can be dangerous. Consumption without optionality invites overcommitment; expression without substance invites regression or social backlash. If you are going to project wealth or power, you must be prepared and capable of defending yourself against those who will challenge your expression. Few things in life are more damaging to one’s sense of self-worth than humiliation. This occurs whenever you experience social regression, perceived or realized. Buying a house or car you cannot afford, and then downgrading later, is humiliating because it projects failure. Attempting to avoid this by borrowing, or doing things you otherwise would not—or legally should not—to maintain status, is a common fate for those who mismanage expressive luxury.

Gradients and Diminishing Returns

Luxury obeys the law of diminishing returns. Early increments bring high satisfaction or optionality, but we always adapt to our environment. What was luxury yesterday seems like necessity today. With each step up, costs rise, and personal benefit shrinks. Beyond a certain point, adding more starts providing negative utility and often more burden.

For example, a home, a car, or leisure resources beyond basic requirements can start to require attention, effort, or maintenance that exceeds their benefit. When you spend more time driving a Ferrari to the shop than enjoying it, the relationship has inverted.

The “sweet spot” is highly individual, but the principle is universal: maximize freedom, minimize burden, and manage for optionality.

Appendix: Tensor Formalization

The tensor space is defined over five orthogonal axes, each a continuous gradient derived from the paper's key truths:

  • R: Resources (surplus beyond needs, capturing quantity and quality excess).
  • T: Time (availability and flexibility, inversely related to maintenance burdens).
  • C: Consumptive value (personal satisfaction, private utility).
  • E: Expressive value (social signaling, visibility and impact).
  • O: Optionality (freedom to deploy or reallocate without coercion or inversion).

in this 5D space, initialized symbolically at a zero-centered equilibrium (no commitments, maximal flexibility, neutral utility). This represents the "empty" lattice before instantiating specific luxury objects (e.g., a house, car, or leisure activity):

\[ \mathbf{L_0} = \begin{bmatrix} R_0 \\ T_0 \\ C_0 \\ E_0 \\ O_0 \end{bmatrix} \]

Where initial placeholders are symbolic parameters (to be instantiated based on context):

  • \( R_0 = r \) (base surplus, e.g., \( r > 0 \) for luxury existence).
  • \( T_0 = t \) (base time flexibility, e.g., \( t \to \infty \) for unconstrained seed).
  • \( C_0 = 0 \) (neutral personal satisfaction, grows with consumption).
  • \( E_0 = 0 \) (neutral signaling, activates with expression).
  • \( O_0 = 1 \) (normalized maximal optionality in empty space).

This seed \(\mathbf{L_0}\) acts as the origin for projections: any luxury object is a vector \(\mathbf{L}\) offset from \(\mathbf{L_0}\), with components scaled by individual context (e.g., \( \mathbf{L} = \mathbf{L_0} + \Delta \mathbf{v} \), where \(\Delta \mathbf{v}\) encodes specific attributes).

Operators on the Tensor

These define dynamic interactions, evolution, and constraints. They transform \(\mathbf{L}\) over time or under conditions, capturing temporal nature, diminishing returns, and burdens.

  1. Decay Operator (D): Models value erosion (e.g., maintenance exceeding benefit, inversion to burden). A diagonal matrix applied element-wise:
\[ \mathbf{D} = \begin{bmatrix} d_R & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & d_T & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & d_C & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & d_E & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & d_O \end{bmatrix} \]

Where \( d_i \in [0,1] \) are axis-specific decay rates (e.g., \( d_E < d_C \) since signaling decays faster than personal satisfaction). Evolution: \( \mathbf{L}(t+1) = \mathbf{D} \cdot \mathbf{L}(t) \).

If \( d_i < 1 \), repeated application enforces diminishing returns.

  1. Growth Operator (G): Captures compounding value (e.g., familiarity increasing utility). A vector function:
\[ \mathbf{G}(t) = g(t) \odot \mathbf{L}(t) \]

Where \( g(t) = [g_R(t), g_T(t), g_C(t), g_E(t), g_O(t)] \), and each \( g_i(t) \) is a growth function (e.g., logarithmic for back-loaded value: \( g_C(t) = \ln(1 + \alpha t) \), \(\alpha > 0\)). Full update: \( \mathbf{L}(t+1) = \mathbf{D} \cdot \mathbf{L}(t) + \mathbf{G}(t) \).

  1. Optionality Constraint (M): A mask tensor enforcing feasibility (e.g., no luxury if resources insufficient). Hadamard product:
\[ \mathbf{M} = \begin{bmatrix} m_R \\ m_T \\ m_C \\ m_E \\ m_O \end{bmatrix}, \quad m_i = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if constraint satisfied (e.g., } R \geq N\text{)} \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \]

Effective luxury: \( \mathbf{L_{eff}} = \mathbf{M} \odot \mathbf{L} \).

  1. Utility Function (U): Scalar projection of the tensor, balancing consumptive/expressive duality and burdens. Weighted sum with penalty:
\[ U(\mathbf{L_{eff}}) = \beta (\mathbf{w_C} \cdot \mathbf{L_{eff}}) + (1-\beta) (\mathbf{w_E} \cdot \mathbf{L_{eff}}) - \gamma \| \mathbf{C_{comp}} \| \]
  • \(\beta \in [0,1]\): Weight between consumptive (\(\beta \to 1\)) and expressive (\(\beta \to 0\)).
  • \(\mathbf{w_C}, \mathbf{w_E}\): Projection vectors (e.g., \(\mathbf{w_C} = [0,0,1,0,0]\) emphasizes C-axis).
  • \(\gamma > 0\): Burden penalty, \(\mathbf{C_{comp}}\): Computed component (e.g., maintenance cost vector).

To incorporate diminishing returns globally: \( U(\mathbf{L_{eff}}) = k \ln(1 + \|\mathbf{L_{eff}}\|) - \gamma \| \mathbf{C_{comp}} \| \), \( k > 0 \).

Optimization

\[ \max_{\mathbf{L}} U(\mathbf{L_{eff}}) \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \mathbf{M} \odot \mathbf{L} = \mathbf{L}, \quad \mathbf{L} \succeq \mathbf{L_0} \]

Instantiation Example

For a "car" object (from paper examples): Start from seed \(\mathbf{L_0}\), apply offsets:

\[ \mathbf{L_{car}} = \mathbf{L_0} + \begin{bmatrix} \Delta R = 5000 - N \\ \Delta T = -10 \text{ (hours/year maintenance)} \\ \Delta C = 0.8 \text{ (satisfaction scale)} \\ \Delta E = 0.6 \text{ (signaling scale)} \\ \Delta O = -0.2 \text{ (reduced flexibility)} \end{bmatrix} \]

Apply operators over time to simulate evolution (e.g., decay turns positive C to burden if O < 0).
