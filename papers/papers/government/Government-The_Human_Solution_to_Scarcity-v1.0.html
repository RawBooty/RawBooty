Government: The Human Solution to The Problem of Scarcity

Government is a hard-to-understand concept.

This is not an accident; it is a design feature of the language we use to talk about it.

This document is not a Republican vs Democrat, left vs right, or any other partisan argument. It provides no consideration for the design of government type or any specific solution. We are not advocating for or against any system. We are simply describing the bare structural mechanics of what government actually is and why it emerges — stripped of all moral, ethical, ideological, and social overlay.

This is the foundation. All moral and ethical considerations, all political preferences, and all proposed solutions should be applied on top of this framework, not before or within it. Once you see the mechanics clearly, you can decide what to do with them.

What few people ever ponder is the nature of life itself.

Life is a fragile state of managed scarcity.

Everything you see, hear, consume, or experience is scarce: time, food, water, air, energy, attention — all are finite. When managed correctly — that is to say, when we consume less than the universe produces — then the system is stable. This is a state of biosis.

The opposite state is dysbiosis: this is where consumption exceeds production, or where productive capacity is destroyed. In this state, the system becomes unstable, fragile, or collapses. In biology, dysbiosis describes an imbalanced microbiome leading to disease. In human systems, it describes any imbalance where scarcity becomes acute because the group consumes or degrades more than it renews.

Absent education and regulatory structure, all organisms by nature will drift toward a state of dysbiosis. They will overconsume until failure. Humans are no different. It is for this very reason that we invented government structures. At its most basic level, government is simply a business that sets rules to delay or prevent dysbiosis, ensuring we manage scarcity in a way that supports the group's long-term sustainability.

Now, let’s be crystal clear. Calling government a "business" will sound strange or even offensive to many people.

This reaction is no accident; it is by careful social design, and it’s exactly why we use the term “government”. Recall from other papers: a business is simply a group of people organized around a common purpose. Government meets this definition perfectly. To the point: government is a class of business.

The reason the two feel morally opposed is propaganda.

Incidentally, “propaganda” is just the intentional loading of language to bias perception — a tool of policy to make one thing sound good and another bad, even when the underlying mechanics are the same.

Over time, "business" has been loaded to mean profit-seeking, greed, and selfishness, while "government" has been loaded to mean caregiver, protector, and public servant. Neither framing is accurate.

Both are the same thing: coordinated groups of people managing resources and responsibilities. They simply exist at different levels of the social structural hierarchy.

Business operates at the level of voluntary exchange and mutual benefit. Government operates at the level of enforced allocation and collective coordination. The moral difference is an illusion created by language and political necessity.

Once we strip away that illusion, we can see government clearly for what it is: a business with a monopoly on force, tasked with managing scarcity at scale when smaller groups can no longer do so through trust or religion.

Business is voluntary. Its power is subordinate.
This is not opinion — it is fact.

Government defines what "business" is allowed to be: through licenses, permits, regulations, taxes, and penalties. Government designs the contract that officially instantiates “business”. Government is compulsory. It takes without asking, and it alone decides what is taken and why.

Government becomes unstable the moment its citizens view taxation — or any compulsory consumption of their production — as theft.

The solution is propaganda: reframe non-voluntary allocation as "necessary," "moral," or "just."

Now apply the same logic to business.

Buying fruit keeps the shopkeeper alive. The shopkeeper profit is why fruit exists on the shelf. Is that profit any less moral or necessary than government receipts?

In this moment, the moralization is revealed.

This is precisely where the paper stops. You must answer this question for yourself.

To understand how government emerged, we must start from the very beginning — with just one person — and build step by step as the number of people grows.

The Emergence Timeline

One Person: No Conflict, No Structure

With one person, there is no conflict. Resources are uncontested. Survival is lonely and fragile, but there is no fight over resources because there is no one else. Possession is absolute and temporary — what you consume is "owned" completely; what you don't is just held. No need for rules or enforcement. Stability is high, but survivorship is low due to nature's pressures: weather, disease, and predation.

Two People: Choice and Limited Conflict

In a society of only two people, co-dependence is not automatic — it is a choice. They can cooperate (symbiosis), one can exploit the other (dependence), or they can live parallel lives with minimal interaction (independence together). Conflict is possible, but it is immediate and personal. They have choices. They either cooperate or one eliminates the other. No anonymity; trust is direct. Stability is binary: ally or eliminate. No codified structure needed.

Small Groups (a Few Dozen People): Complexity Begins

In small groups — the size of a typical hunter-gatherer band — everyone knows everyone. Trust is personal, reputation matters, and conflict can still be handled face-to-face or through family ties. Stability is maintained through kin reciprocity and shame, but as numbers grow, interactions increase, and friction builds with natural increases in complexity.

Religion Emerges: Fear as the First Control Surface

Religion is the first true control surface that humans invented to manage group behavior when personal trust and direct reciprocity start to fail.

At small scales (roughly a few dozen people — the size of early hunter-gatherer bands), everyone knows everyone. Reputation, kinship, and immediate consequences can still keep conflict in check. But even at this size, volatility is brutal: weather, disease, predators, resource scarcity, and injury can wipe out a group in one bad season. Survival odds are low without ways to enforce self-control and group cohesion.

Religion solves this by introducing fear of the unknown — the dark, monsters, spirits, gods, or an afterlife with consequences. These are not arbitrary superstitions; they are selected heuristics that bias behavior toward long-term group survival.

Examples:

  • Don't eat pork → hygiene script (prevents trichinosis or other diseases in hot climates).

  • Don't mix meat and milk → food safety heuristic (reduces bacterial cross-contamination).

  • Delayed gratification rules → mental models for planting now, harvesting later.

  • Taboos against killing kin → preserves group cohesion.

Religion works because it is low-cost, high-leverage: it doesn't require constant physical enforcement. Fear of an invisible watcher (one who sees everything, even when no human does) creates internal compliance. People self-regulate because the perceived risk of supernatural punishment is high, and it's easily avoided by following the rules. (parents will use Santa or the Tooth Fairy in similar ways to bias young children’s behavior)

This is the first time humans delegate enforcement to something outside the immediate group. The "eyes" are not human — they are omnipresent and infallible. That makes the bias extremely powerful.

Religion stabilizes small groups up to the agrarian shift. It allows people to live in larger bands than pure kinship could support, because fear scales better than personal reputation.

But religion has limits. It is still rooted in belief and ritual. When populations grow further and anonymity increases, fear of gods becomes harder to sustain across strangers. Supernatural enforcement starts to weaken for people who don't share the same stories or rituals. At that point, the system needs a new layer — something more impersonal, more standardized, and backed by visible force.

Religion is not the end-state. It is the bridge from personal/tribal control to institutional control. It buys time until scale demands the next fork: government.

Key primitive: Fear-based information bias

Religion is the original technology that uses narrative (stories of gods, monsters, afterlife) to shape perception and enforce behavior without constant physical intervention. It is the first control surface that works at small-group scale, but it is fragile when anonymity erodes shared belief. Government inherits and scales this tool: from supernatural fear to state narrative to perceived risk of punishment.

This is why early religious codes look like survival manuals disguised as divine commands.

They were.

And they worked — until they didn't.

Agrarian Shift: Scarcity and Self-Control

The shift from hunting and gathering to farming and herding is one of the most consequential changes in human history. It marks the moment when scarcity becomes acute and self-control becomes non-negotiable.

Hunter-gatherers live in a world of immediate rewards. Food is found or killed today and eaten today. There is no planting, no waiting months for a harvest. Take the reward now, because tomorrow is uncertain. Groups stay small, mobile, and relatively egalitarian because hoarding is impractical — you can’t carry a surplus of roots or game on your back for long.

With agriculture comes new problems. You plant seeds today and hope for food months later. You herd animals and must protect them from predators and raiders while waiting for them to grow or reproduce. This is a completely different game: delayed gratification is no longer optional — it is required for survival.

The problem is immediate. If you eat the seed corn today, there is no crop tomorrow. If you slaughter the breeding stock, the herd dies out. Self-control — the ability to forgo consumption now for future payoff — becomes the difference between life and death. This is especially hard in times of famine or drought.

Religion already provides some of the tools: taboos, parables, and fear of supernatural punishment encourage restraint. “Don’t eat the seed” can be framed as “the gods will curse you if you do.” But fear alone starts to fail when the group gets larger and resources become more contested. Cheating becomes easier: one person eats the seed corn, another slaughters a breeding animal. The group starves while the cheater gains short-term advantage, hiding behind anonymity or plausible deniability.

This is where ownership begins to emerge — first socially, then religiously. Resources are no longer “everyone’s” in the same way. Land is claimed, herds are marked, seed stores are guarded. Ownership is initially “god-owned” or group-owned, with religious leaders or elders as stewards. The fear of divine punishment is used to enforce the new rules: “This land belongs to the gods; steal from it and suffer their wrath.”

But even this starts to crack as populations grow and land becomes scarce. Tribe-tribe conflicts intensify: virgin territory runs out, and groups must fight over the same fields, pastures, and water sources. Competitive systems collide — the group with better organization, better enforcement of ownership, or better ability to coordinate wins. The losers die, flee, or are absorbed.

The agrarian shift is the point where the need for structure becomes unavoidable. Delayed rewards require self-control. Self-control requires enforcement. Enforcement at scale requires rules, measurement, and punishment beyond what religion and kinship can provide. The seeds of government are planted here — not as a moral vision, but as a practical necessity to prevent the otherwise inevitable dysbiosis.

Anonymity Threshold: The Break Point

Once the group grows larger — beyond the size where everyone personally knows everyone — anonymity becomes unavoidable. Strangers appear in the mix. You no longer know who is trustworthy, and exploitation becomes possible without immediate social cost.

The mechanisms that worked in small groups now start to fail. Religion and tribal norms rely on personal accountability: reputation, kinship, and fear of visible consequences. When anonymity hides bad actors, the old heuristics lose their power.

This is the break point. The system can no longer self-regulate through trust or shared belief. Something new is needed to manage the resulting resource conflict and prevent collapse.

Tragedy of the Commons: The Trigger

Shared resources deplete when anonymity aligns self-interest against the group (Hardin, 1968). No one owns, everyone exploits, collapse follows. This is the specific problem government resolves — not "structuring society," but managing scarcity under anonymity.

Ownership as Solution: Delegated Agency

Ownership emerges as the fiction for temporary possession: limited agency over resources to prevent tragedy. You "own" what you delegate responsibility for (control, maintenance, risk), but it's not absolute — only "true" for complete consumption (food, fuel). Title is legal mumbo-jumbo for transferal, backed by punishment. Government codifies this (boundaries, units of measure for land/livestock), turning commons into excluded assets.

Enforcement as Mechanism: Third-Party Resolution

Ownership creates disputes — who gets what, who owes what, who violated what. Personal vengeance and direct retaliation work in small groups, but they don't scale. Revenge spirals into feuds, and strangers can't be trusted to self-police.

Third-party enforcement emerges as the solution: an impersonal, standardized body that judges breaches and applies punishment. It injects distance — the resolver is not a family member or neighbor with a personal stake, but a neutral authority.

Punishment (fines, revocation of rights, restraint, or worse) is not always carried out. It doesn't need to be. The perceived risk of punishment is usually enough to bias behavior toward compliance. The "eyes of the state" — like cameras, laws, or even the simple knowledge that someone is watching — do most of the work. People self-regulate because the cost of being caught is high and avoidable.

Enforcement is seldom necessary in full. Fear of consequences is all that's needed most of the time.

This is why government scales: it turns personal risk into impersonal risk, making large groups stable enough to function.

Monopoly on Force: The Core Primitive

Government is defined by its perceived dominance in building, managing, and deploying force — legitimate violence, restraint, and intimidation.

This is not an absolute monopoly (e.g., distributed arms or private security can check it), but it is credible enough to shift behavior.

The credible threat of force is what matters: people comply not because force is always used, but because they believe it will be used if they cross the line. The risk of punishment — fines, imprisonment, loss of property, or worse — is high enough and avoidable enough to act as a bias. Force is seldom necessary in full. The perception of force is usually sufficient.

This is why government works at scale: it turns personal risk (fear of kin or gods) into impersonal, omnipresent risk. The "eyes of the state" — laws, police, courts, cameras — create a gradient of perceived consequences that modifies behavior without constant violence.

Death or loss is the ultimate choice, but government's monopoly makes non-compliance feel high-risk and avoidable — and that perception is the real lever.

What makes government a special class of business is this specific power. In almost every other capacity, the two are indistinguishable.

Semantics Corruption: The Propaganda Layer

We find ourselves in this state of mass confusion about what government is and should be doing because information is the primary control layer for government.

The easiest way to organize and regulate behavior is through selective modification of words to create useful meaning.

After thousands of years of government, we are at a point where, realistically, all useful terms have been corrupted to sanitize power: "ownership" is now seen as a right instead of fiction; "title" as truth instead of human fantasy; "law" as justice instead of coercion; and "tax" as accepted contribution instead of enforced extraction.

This is not to say any of this is bad. These terms are phrased this way to illustrate definitional bias only. Peel back the layers of etymology and philology, and you can discover the history of these terms and see how they evolved. Even "booty" was born as propaganda. The irony of it all.

The point being: we choose to create government. We choose to delegate authority. We do this because it is necessary and mutually beneficial.

The Compulsory Nature of Government: Core Powers and Surrendered Choice

Government is a voluntary consolidation of power to set rules — yes, but more importantly, a monopoly granted to one group to define and administer punishment for non-compliance.

If I lock you in a cage in the basement for a year, this is cruel and morally reprehensible. But if the central authority does it, it is considered necessary and just.

The same for making war, or any of the innumerable powers people surrender to government.

We do this for a simple reason: society only functions when there is predictability.

Ever wonder why you can drive your car down a road at 60 mph? Because you know all other people in cars will stay in their lane. Two vehicles weighing 6,000 lbs heading toward each other at 60 mph with only 6' spacing would seem like a very high-risk situation. Yet it happens all over the world all the time.

Why? Because we all know crossing the line is deadly. Who drew the line? Who designed the line? What do you do when someone decides to drive in the wrong lane? You do nothing, because you delegated that responsibility to the traffic police. They literally police traffic. To police means, definitionally, to enforce rules.
