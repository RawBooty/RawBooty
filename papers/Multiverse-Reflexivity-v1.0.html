The Birth of Multiverse Reflexivity

Why Bubbles Last Longer (But Still Burst)

Over roughly the last ~decade, the markets have become increasingly unsettling.

The cycles I studied, trained on, and experienced professionally no longer behave the way they used to. Yes, bubbles still form, but they no longer end cleanly. Instead of rising, peaking, and collapsing, they now partially deflate, re-inflate, persisting far longer than expected, or worse yet bleeding into adjacent narratives seeding smaller secondary bubbles. What looks like a terminal phase often turns out to be an intermission. What used to be a single clean bubble, looks increasing like a bubble cluster.

This phenomenon can be observed everywhere: crypto $btc, electric vehicles $tsla, AI $NVDA, precious metals $SLV $GLD, space $ASTS. Valuations that once would have been all but impossible, now not only exist, but seem to persist for years. Even when faced with deteriorating fundamentals, prices drift inexorably higher. As for the corrections, if you want to call them that, when they do arrive, they are often sharp, shallow and short-lived. *Something* keeps re-animating the cycle.

The obvious explanation is “inflation” but this explanation is woefully incomplete. Yes inflation may explain nominal price drift, but it does not explain duration, resilience, or repeated renewal. Something structural has changed.

What follows is my attempt to explain what that change is.

Bubbles have always existed, our focus should be on their duration.

Before addressing what’s new, it helps to ground what is not.

At the most basic level, a company is simply a legal structure that organizes people around a common purpose. For most of history, ownership of such structures was private: held by individuals or small partnerships. The creation of public equity markets was effectively a socialization (democratization) of ownership, allowing claims on future cash flows to be distributed widely across a large group of principals.

For much of market history, the incentive to own equities was straightforward: pro-rata participation in cash flows via dividends. Over time, particularly following the introduction of capital gains taxation and changes in corporate governance, incentives shifted away from payout and toward reinvestment and price appreciation. Gradually, price came to dominate cash flow as the primary signal.

Despite this evolution, one thing remained surprisingly stable: the cycle. Bubbles formed, inflated, and collapsed; typically over a horizon of three to five years. Excesses always corrected. Narratives exhausted themselves. Capital rotated.

Reflexivity in a Unified Market

Financial markets began as private transactions, then evolved into open outcry auctions in centralized physical locations. Later came telephones, electronic exchanges, and finally the internet. With each technological advance, information moved faster and transaction costs fell and access grew. But despite these changes, markets still functioned as a largely unified arena.

Conceptually, it is useful to imagine the market as a single surface. Something like a shared plane of attention and price discovery.

It was within this context that George Soros formally articulated the concept of reflexivity. The idea is simple: an external catalyst draws attention to a company or asset. Attention increases demand. Demand pushes price higher. Rising prices generate news. News draws further attention. The price action itself becomes a marketing mechanism.

This feedback loop—news → attention → demand → price → more news—drives bubbles.

In a unified market surface, this loop behaves like a wave moving across calm water. It expands until one of three things happens: momentum slows, attention saturates, or a cross-current disrupts the flow.

When that happens, the loop breaks down, and the bubble collapses.

Historically, this process produced relatively predictable lifespans.

The Structural Break: Fragmentation of Attention

So why do cycles now appear to last a decade or more?

The answer is both obvious and counterintuitive.

The obvious part is that markets have fragmented. More precisely, the surface has fractured.

The single most important catalyst for this fracture is the commoditization of the smartphone. Smartphones mobilized information, driving its marginal cost toward zero while making access omnipresent and real-time. Layered on top of this came zero-commission trading, fractional shares, and instant access to leverage through derivatives.

Put simply the historical barriers to entry collapsed to near zero. Nearly everyone on planet earth can now, research and trade stocks and other financial instruments from their phone from anywhere on earth in real time.

At the same time, market participation has shifted dramatically. Where markets were once dominated by slow-moving institutional capital (entities constrained by fiduciary duty, liquidity requirements, and career risk) they are now heavily influenced by retail participation. Retail traders act without fiduciary obligation, without liability, and without a requirement for diligence. Individually they are small and largely meaningless participants; collectively, their actions can overwhelm..

Paradoxically, all this increase in access has caused liquidity to thin. Lot sizes shrank. Micro-transactions have proliferated and market depth has vanished.

1980 average lot size was 500-2000 shares

1990 average lot size was 500-1000

2000 average lot size was 400-700 (post-decimalization)

2010 average lot size was 200-400 (HFT, reg NMS, electronic fragmentation)

2020 average lot size is 100-200 (zero commission, fractional shares, etc..)

This converts markets from capital-weighted systems into attention-weighted ones. This alone meaningfully alters market dynamics; but it is not the core of the story.

The deeper change came from social media.

Markets No Longer Share a Single Arena

To unpack this dynamic it’s important to understand that social media is not organized around finance. It is instead organized around interests, identity, and social networks. Additionally, financial information ultimately affects everyone, so it does not stay in its lane. When a signal is strong enough, financial news will radiate outwards, infecting adjacent communities that were never seeking it out.

It’s also important to bear in mind that each major platform operates as its own universe of attention. For example: X behaves like a real-time stream of collective consciousness. TikTok is a high-velocity river of short-form video. Reddit is a slower collection of niche, self-reinforcing communities. YouTube is a vast, poorly navigated archive of long-form narratives. And there are many other large platforms.

Importantly, each platform has its own culture, population, and algorithmic biases. Within each platform, users are further segmented into tightly quarantined silos. These silos behave like distinct attention surfaces, each with its own decay rate, amplification bias, and saturation threshold. They are akin to little self-contained microcosms, or universes.

This self-isolation is practical. Creators, constrained by the labor intensity of quality production, typically operate on one or two platforms. As a result, crossovers are sparse. Intersections occur only when algorithms align or narratives mutate just enough to escape their original container.

The result is not one market surface, but many. A fragmented map of attention composed of partially overlapping, desynchronized planes.

When Reflexivity Went Trans-Surface

This is where classical reflexivity breaks down.

The reflexive dynamics I learned early in my career assumed a unified surface. A single dominant feedback loop. A shared decay process.

This assumption no longer works.

Reflexive signals are no longer confined to one plane. They now propagate across multiple, misaligned attention surfaces. The best way I’ve found to visualize this is not as a wave, but as an amorphous mass floating through space.

When a catalyst ignites reflexivity within one attention plane, it begins to accumulate momentum, pulling participants in through FOMO, transaction velocity, and narrative reinforcement. In a classical market, this mass would eventually lose coherence and collapse on saturation.

In the current environment, something different happens.

As the reflexive mass approaches saturation in one plane, it leaks into adjacent planes through cross-posting, algorithmic promotion, or narrative mutation. In transit, it deforms. It stretches, splits, and reconstitutes; less like a wave, more like a droplet in zero gravity.

Momentum does not decay uniformly. It renews unevenly across segregated planes. What appears as exhaustion in one universe becomes ignition in another. The relevant variable is no longer local momentum, but something closer to a weighted, cross-plane gamma: an aggregate sensitivity to attention that is dynamic, path-dependent, and effectively unobservable in real time.

Even after a cycle completes in a given plane, that same plane can be reseeded later with a variant of the old narrative, reviving curiosity among a new cohort. The mass does not die. It limps on, anchored elsewhere.

Sharp pullbacks do still occur. These are often moments of decoupling. Local liquidity drains faster than attention can replenish it causing prices snap back. But the broader reflexive object stubbornly persists.

The result is not a clean cycle, but a fractal descent that can linger for years.

The Enabling Conditions

Several structural forces enable this regime.

Liquidity thinning means prices are increasingly set by marginal trades rather than committed capital. Margin capacity is determined by the last trade, regardless of size. As lot sizes shrink, price becomes more detached from enterprise value.

Narrative dominance has grown to largely replace fundamentals. For popular names, the investment is no longer in cash flows, but in attention. Stories with meme value are seemingly unconstrained. Capital requirements to push prices higher shrink as prices rise, creating a self-reinforcing loop.

Institutions and algorithmic traders understand this dynamic. They do not fight it; they harvest it. Retail flow becomes both fuel and prey.

Retail traders, once irrelevant, now behave like a swarm of locusts. Individually inconsequential, yet collectively overwhelming. They largely move without coordination, without liability, and without time horizons. In many ways they move without hesitation and their emotional responses, particularly FOMO, amplify reflexive dynamics far beyond historical precedent.

What we have is an entirely new model of price discovery.

The New Timeline

The bottom line is this: we are operating on a new timeline. Historical analogs do not exist.

Reflexivity itself has not changed. What has changed is the structure it propagates through.

The classical formulation assumed a largely unified market surface: one dominant arena of attention, one primary feedback loop, one shared decay process. That definition is now too flat, too one dimensional. Attention is now fragmented across many parallel channels, each with its own dynamics and populations. Signal propagation across these channels, critically, is multiphasic.

What we are experiencing behaves more like weather systems.

No, bubbles are not new. What is new is their size, duration, and durability. They will still collapse, just under different conditions than we are accustomed to looking for.

What is clear is that valuation no longer serves as a reliable signal. Social engagement (gamma) may matter more.

For this reason, I find it useful to reframe reflexivity as something slightly different:

Multiverse Reflexivity (or reflexivityⁿ):
a regime in which feedback loops propagate across fragmented, desynchronized attention systems, extending duration and delaying saturation without eliminating collapse.

This reframing does not make bubbles permanent. It simply makes them harder to time, more resilient to disruption, and more dangerous to dismiss.

It also explains why, in this environment, being early increasingly feels indistinguishable from being wrong.
